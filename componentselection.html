Individual Component Selection

Here I have my individual component selection. Included are both my considered options for all major subsystems, as well as my justification for choosing these components. Due to project requirements, through-hole components will not be considered for selection.

Major Hardware Selection

Microcontroller Selection

For microcontroller selection, I am prioritizing familiarity and ease of programming. Utilizing a microcontroller that supports C libraries gives the widest range of potential components compared to other available languages, as well as its use within most major microcontroller brands.

Component Image Advantages Disadvantages Hyperlink
PIC18F46K40-I/PT
  • Familiarity with programming interface
  • Inexpensive
  • Plentiful documentation
  • Teaching-team support
  • Likely to be popular amongst students (Better community support)
  • Higher internal clock (64MHz)
  • Although efficient within the 8-bit realm, its computational capabilities and memory size are limited when compared with modern 32-bit microcontrollers that offer higher performance and greater memory capacity.
  • Might be a little much based on the project requirements
  • Restricted access to python libraries without manual labor
PIC18F46K22
  • Good balance of power & efficiency
  • nanoWatt XLP (Extreme Low Power) technology, making it great for battery-operated applications.
  • Larger Flash than PIC18F4520 (64 KB vs. 32 KB).
  • Better peripherals than the PIC18F4520 (more ADC channels, PWM, and timers).
  • Less flexibility with Peripheral functions like UART, SPI, and PWM.
  • Lacks some advanced features like CIP & CRC.
  • Limited documentation/support compared to older PICs
PIC18F4520
  • Simple & widely used, making it easy to find documentation & support.
  • Lower cost than the others.
  • Basic peripherals sufficient for simpler applications.
  • 5V operation, making it good for direct interface with TTL components.
  • Smallest Flash (32 KB) & RAM (1.5 KB).
  • Slower clock speed (40 MHz max vs. 64 MHz on others).
  • Limited peripherals (only 1 UART, 1 I2C, 1 SPI, fewer ADC & PWM channels).

My Selection

Considering this microcontroller is widely used it will give access to a wide knowledge base, and the labs we do leading up to the final project will help develop my skills with this specific controller
Power Regulator Selection

For power regulator selection, I am prioritizing ease of use and minimal support components. This will maximize the time I have to work on more interesting subsystems, and reduce the risk of component failure. Ideally a fixed-output regulator for ease-of-use. It also must be capable of producing the power required to drive the microcontroller and motor driver logic circuit.

Component Image Advantages Disadvantages Hyperlink
LM338T-NOPB
  • Reliable
  • Used through-hole version in previous course
  • Minimal supporting hardware
  • Fixed output at microcontroller voltage using external components
  • Excellent documentation and customer support
  • Significantly more expensive than alternatives
  • Prides itself on its 5A current supply but that is more than needed.
  • Many NC Pins contribute to footprint, but not functionality
LM1084-IS
  • The LM1084 is designed as a low-dropout (LDO) regulator. This means it can maintain regulation with a smaller difference between the input and output voltage.
  • Inexpensive
  • Reduced design complexity.
  • Limited to 1.5A maximum current.
  • Dropout voltage is moderate, meaning it may require a higher input voltage margin.
MIC29301 fixed regulator
  • Higher current capability (up to about 3A) with a much lower dropout voltage.
  • Improved efficiency when the input voltage is close to the output voltage.
  • Can typically supply up to around 3A.
  • The MIC29301 is designed with modern low-dropout design techniques that often improve transient response and overall stability at higher currents.
  • The improved dropout and higher current rating can demand careful thermal management if used near its maximum current.
  • Might be more costly and slightly more complex than simpler designs.

My Selection

Component Image Justification
LM1084IS-3.3NOPB The LM1084 can provide up to 1.5A of current, which is perfect for the microcontroller. It offers simplicity and ease-of-use, making it ideal for when the load is within its current rating and I can afford the dropout voltage margin.
Motor Driver Selection

For motor driver selection, Their are many limitation due to many of these motor drivers being pre-mounted onto daughter-boards, which require justification to use (As per the project requirements). Ideally the control logic is at the same voltage as the microcontroller. It also requires control over a serial communication interface, which doesn't seem like a difficult task, but it is actually rather difficult. Many allow tuning properties, such as acceleration speed over a serial interface, but operate using a pulse-step system.

  • Continuous Current of 43A and a max current of 70A

  • Voltage range of 5.5V tp 24V

  • Chopper current limitation for improved motor control.
  • Good protection mechanisms (short circuit, overtemperature, open load detection).
  • Compact package with excellent thermal management.
  • Lower current capability (6A continuous vs. 12A on VNH5019).

My Selection

Component Image Justification
IFX9201SGAUMA1 At this moment, Im thinking that IFX9201SGAUMA1 motor driver will go well with the motor i'll be using. The motor has a rated current of 1.5A, which is well below the 6A continuous rating of the motor driver, ensuring safe operation. The PIC18F46K40 supports SPI communication, making it compatible with the IFX9201SG for advanced diagnostics.
Motor Selection

In my subsystem, the motor is where the magic happens. I'm looking for DC motor that I can control with my PIC18F46K40 microcontroller and the IFX9201SG motor driver but also needs enough voltage to accomplish the renactment of a wind tunnel.

Component Image Advantages Disadvantages Hyperlink
70:1 METAL GEARMOTOR 37DX70L MM
  • This brushed DC motor operates at voltages up to 60V and offers a peak torque of up to 240 oz-in.
  • Designed for applications requiring robust torque performance and features a rugged, heavy-gauge steel housing for durability.
  • More than enough torque for my application
  • Designed for hobbyists, seems to have a relatively high manufacturing failure rate
  • Round shaft, so set screw will be required to attach to hardware
STANDARD MOTOR 3700 RPM 24V
  • Typically more affordable, making it suitable for budget-conscious projects.
  • Offers a high rotational speed of 3700 RPM, ideal for applications where speed is prioritized over torque.
  • Absence of an encoder or feedback mechanism makes it less suitable for applications requiring precise control.
  • Relatively low torque may restrict other design considerations
ROB-15277
  • Very popular for hobbyist applications, plenty of online support
  • Reliable
  • Combines motor and gearbox into a single unit, simplifying installation and reducing the overall footprint.
  • Larger than some alternatives
  • More expensive than some alternatives

My Selection

Component Image Justification
ROB-15277 Component familiarity combined with access to years of online discussion makes this the superior choice. The familiarity has immense value to me, especially given the complexity of the motor driver selected.Our application demands a compact motor with integrated high torque and speed control, and budget is not a primary concern. I would rather the motor itself be the easiest part to integrate.
